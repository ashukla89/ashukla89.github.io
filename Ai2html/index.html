San Francisco Has Exceptional Running Weather

Other popular cities for California expats fall well short.

By ASEEM SHUKLA

Published September 9, 2019

By now, everyone knows that people are leaving San Francisco in droves. The sheer expense, the tech monoculture, NIMBYism, and rampant homelessness and filth have all conspired to turn a onetime bohemian paradise into a barely livable playground for the rich.

But outdoor fitness junkies who leave the City may want to think twice about their decision. Climate data suggests that exercising outdoors just isn't as pleasant in the cities people leave SF for.

Where are San Franciscans going?

According to research by Trulia, the following five cities are those that Californians (admittedly not San Franciscans, specifically) leave the state for:

  • New York
  • Las Vegas
  • Phoenix
  • Dallas
  • Seattle

And what does it mean for running weather to be “good?”

Admittedly this is a bit subjective, but science does have something to say on the matter. Reporters at the Elite Daily have determined that the optimal temperature for running long-distance is 49-50 F, and for sprinting it’s 72-73 F. Since all runs are, by definition, somewhere between a sprint and long-distance run, we’ll call those our bookends and include all temperatures in between.

We can also assume that nobody likes running in the rain, so we'll exclude rainy days too.

In other words, good running days are:

  • Those with high temps between 50 and 72.5.
  • Those with no rain recorded.

How does running weather by city compare?

Given the criteria defined above, we can compare each of the six cities (SF plus the 5 destination cities) by the frequency of "good" running weather year round. The below reflects 39 years of historical data from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA).

To sum up:

  • SF has pretty great running weather year round.
  • Seattle and New York are temperate, with bad running weather in the winter and the summer. The shoulder seasons are pretty okay-- but they’re longer in Seattle, giving Seattle a distinct edge over NYC.
  • Vegas, Phoenix, and Dallas have terrible summers. Their winters are actually great for running, though-- particularly in Vegas.
